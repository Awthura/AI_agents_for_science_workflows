\section*{Overview}

Each conference is scored on three axes; the distance score contributes 30\% to the
weighted total:

\begin{equation}
    s_{\text{total}} = 0.50 \cdot s_{\text{rel}} + 0.30 \cdot s_{\text{dist}} + 0.20 \cdot s_{\text{pres}}
\end{equation}

\section*{Step 1 — Haversine Distance}

Given the researcher's location $(\phi_1, \lambda_1)$ and the conference location
$(\phi_2, \lambda_2)$ in decimal degrees, the great-circle distance $d$ is:

\begin{equation}
    a = \sin^2\!\left(\frac{\phi_2 - \phi_1}{2}\right)
        + \cos\phi_1 \cdot \cos\phi_2 \cdot
          \sin^2\!\left(\frac{\lambda_2 - \lambda_1}{2}\right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    d = 2R \cdot \arcsin\!\left(\sqrt{a}\right), \qquad R = 6371\ \text{km}
\end{equation}

where $\phi$ denotes latitude, $\lambda$ denotes longitude, and $R$ is the mean
Earth radius.

\section*{Step 2 — Distance to Score}

The raw distance $d$ is converted to a score $s_{\text{dist}} \in [0, 100]$ via
exponential decay:

\begin{equation}
    s_{\text{dist}}(d) = 100 \cdot \exp\!\left(-\frac{3d}{d_{\max}}\right),
    \qquad d_{\max} = 15{,}000\ \text{km}
\end{equation}

\noindent Key reference values:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
$d$ (km) & Example & $s_{\text{dist}}$ \\
\hline
0       & Same city          & 100.0 \\
500     & Berlin             &  90.5 \\
1\,906  & Athens             &  68.3 \\
5\,000  & New York           &  36.8 \\
9\,200  & Osaka              &  16.3 \\
15\,000 & Antipode           &   5.0 \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent If geocoding fails for either location, $s_{\text{dist}}$ defaults to $50.0$
(neutral mid-range value).

\section*{Design Rationale}

Exponential decay reflects how researchers perceive travel: the difference between
a local (0 km) and a national (500 km) conference is significant, while the
difference between a long-haul (8\,000 km) and a very long-haul (10\,000 km)
conference is marginal. A linear scale would unfairly penalise all overseas venues.
